◈ MISSION BRIEF
Firewall's Cerean proxy, a data broker called MESA, has gone silent. Her last message just stops — mid-sentence, mid-breath, like a signal that forgot it was transmitting. Her last partial message hinted she'd intercepted something catastrophic — a fragment of a TITAN-class signal echoing from deep-ecliptic space, bounced off Gutenberg Station's relay array. The PCs have travelled to Vesta under commercial cover to recover her dead drop, confirm the signal is real, and establish whether Gutenberg has been compromised. They are booked into the Regolith Arms — a short-stay hotel in the Docking Ring — the same block where MESA stayed under her alias three days ago.
◈ PRIMARY OBJECTIVE Recover MESA's dead drop from Hab 4-Delta-7 in Vesta's Docking Ring District. Authenticate its contents. Survive contact with whoever else is looking for it.
◈ SECONDARY OBJECTIVES Determine MESA's fate. Identify at least one of her pursuers (a Cognite corp-sec team). Plant disinformation in the Vestan mesh to protect Gutenberg's true status.
GM NOTE: The session's structural conceit is a cold open that creates a decision before the players have oriented themselves — someone is breaking into the room next door before a single line of exposition has been delivered. ◈ STARTING FUNDS & REQUISITION

Each sentinel starts with 20 GP personal funds — enough for basics, not enough for comfort. Firewall does not over-equip. It gives a job and expects the rest to be figured out.

In addition, each sentinel has been given a Firewall Requisition Chit — a one-use mesh token worth 25 GP, pre-staged through a local asset. The chit can only be redeemed through Aiko Strand (gear and information) or Devraj Mistry (black market hardware). It cannot be spent before those contacts are made.

The chit's existence means Firewall had assets positioned on Vesta before the sentinels arrived. That's either reassuring or alarming depending on how much the players trust their handler.

◈ HOW THEY RECEIVED THE MISSION — DELIVERY MECHANICS
The sentinels were not briefed in person. There was no handler meeting, no secure dead drop, no voice call. The briefing arrived as an encrypted fork of the Cerean proxy's infomorph — a compressed ego fragment containing only the operational brief — transmitted through a relay node and loaded directly into each sentinel's muse during egocast transit to Vesta. By the time resleeving was complete, the infomorph fork had executed, delivered its payload, and deleted itself. It left no record and no return address.
The muse now holds the operational details: MESA's alias, the hotel booking, the challenge phrase for Aiko and Devraj, the extraction contact aboard the Kessler Patience. Everything the sentinels know, they received in transit. They have never spoken to the Cerean proxy directly and have no mechanism to reach back. Aiko and Orin Deschamps are the only two authorised contact points on Vesta. If something goes wrong that isn't covered in the brief, the sentinels improvise.
WHY NO RETURN CONTACT
Firewall runs lean. Contact threads are vulnerabilities. The proxy who sent the brief doesn't know if it was received until the operation either succeeds or the sentinels egocast off Vesta. That's the design. It's also why MESA's alert was taken seriously — she went dark on a frequency Firewall only uses for one-way emergency signals.
WHAT THE MUSE KNOWS
The briefing as loaded into the muse covers: objectives, MESA's alias and location, commercial cover identities, the challenge phrase, Aiko and Devraj as authorised spend contacts, Orin Deschamps and the Kessler Patience departure window (T+40min from session start). The muse does not know Cognite is involved — that's field intelligence, not pre-mission intel.
WHAT NOBODY TOLD THEM
That Sable Index pre-positioned before MESA disappeared. That VSC-REG-0442 was filed before the sentinels were briefed. That Cognite recognised the Gutenberg frequency from their own Kuiper Belt experiment. That the fork on the data spike is damaged. The proxy either didn't know these things or assessed them as field-discoverable. The sentinels find all of it themselves.
IF PLAYERS ASK WHO SENT IT
The Cerean proxy. That's the routing identifier — whether it's a person operating from Ceres, a mesh node, or a rotating infomorph with no fixed address is deliberately unclear. The proxy's voice was part of the briefing fork. The sentinels know what it sounds like. They have no way to verify whether they'd recognise the same entity if they encountered it again. Firewall doesn't do introductions.
◈ BACKGROUND / SITUATION

MESA (Meridian Echo Signals Aggregator) was an infomorph data broker with no physical morph — she existed as a pure ego distributed across a dozen anonymized shell accounts. Three days ago, while correlating routine deep-space relay logs for a Firewall watcher, she found something she wasn't supposed to: the Gutenberg Station relay was bouncing a signal on a frequency that doesn't exist in any registered protocol. It matched no known system, but had structural similarities to corrupted data packets recovered from TITAN-era research archives.

MESA packaged her findings and initiated a dead drop protocol to a physical location on Vesta — data physically embedded in a cheap piece of ceramic art at Hab 4-Delta-7, which she controls under the alias "Noor Yazdan." Before going dark, she briefly took physical form at the Regolith Arms hotel in the Docking Ring — the same block where Firewall has booked the PCs. This is not a coincidence. Firewall chose it deliberately: MESA's transit records show she used the hotel's abandoned mesh relay node as an emergency extraction point for her ego backup. There may be traces.

A Cognite subsidiary — Sable Index Solutions — was monitoring the same frequency anomaly. They have a three-person corp-sec team on Vesta, working from cover as an industrial salvage assessor firm. They've been running MESA's trail backward through commercial records and have identified the Regolith Arms as a location of interest. They don't know Firewall is here — yet.

◈ ARC III — SESSIONS 07–12

◈ WHAT THEY'RE ACTUALLY MINING — VESTA GEOLOGY
Vesta is one of the few asteroids that fully differentiated — it melted early in solar system history and separated into distinct layers like a planet. This is what makes it worth mining: the iron-nickel isn't scattered through undifferentiated rock. It concentrates where it should, the way it does on worlds. Rimward confirms mining targets as "nickel, iron, and valuable metals" from the differentiated mantle. The geology says exactly what that means.
IRON-NICKEL — PRIMARY COMMERCIAL TARGET
The main commodity. Concentrated at the crust-mantle contact zone and in the metallic core (estimated 90–220km diameter). Structural metal for shipbuilding — which is exactly why Starware positioned its main shipyard facility in Vesta's orbit. The ore coming out of these mines isn't glamorous. It's bulk commodity iron-nickel at industrial scale. The miners are shift workers, not prospectors.
PYROXENE-BEARING ROCK — SECONDARY PRODUCT
The basaltic crust runs 10–20km thick and is layered: surface regolith (howardites), basaltic lava flows (eucrites), then deeper plutonic pyroxene and orthopyroxene-rich rock (diogenites). Processed for ceramics, construction aggregate, radiation shielding, and raw fill material. Aiko's handmade ceramics at the Coppervein Market are almost certainly made from processed pyroxene and basaltic silicate slag — local material, local craft.
OLIVINE — DEEP MANTLE (RHEASILVIA SHAFTS)
The Rheasilvia impact crater at the South Pole punched through the entire crust — 20–40km — and into the upper mantle, exposing olivine (magnesium-iron silicate). Industrial abrasive, refractories, possible platinum-group trace elements in associated rock. The Dweller bought a mining settlement in the Rheasilvia Crater. The deepest, most geologically anomalous location on the asteroid. The settlement with the best access to mantle-depth material. That is not a coincidence.
WHAT VESTA ISN'T — NO GOLD RUSH HERE
Vesta is a V-type asteroid, not an M-type. M-type metallic asteroids (the broken cores of early protoplanets) are the ones worth trillions — pure iron-nickel with platinum, gold, iridium, uranium. V-type means basaltic crust over a differentiated body. There's iron-nickel here, but it's not the El Dorado of the Belt. Vesta's wealth is in volume and logistics position, not in exceptional mineral density. The workers know this. The resentment is built in.

◈ WHAT VESTA FEELS LIKE INSIDE

Vesta is not a constructed station. It is a hollowed and adapted asteroid, which means the interior architecture follows the rock rather than any engineering ideal. The inhabited sections use centrifuge rings carved or bored into the rock at different depths, spinning independently inside the asteroid's body. Gravity depends entirely on which ring you're in and how deep. Nothing is rectangular. The corridors follow the grain of the bore. Rooms are irregular. Ceilings dip. Floors have lips and steps where the boring equipment took a slightly different angle.

THE SMELL
Rock dust, recycled air, and the sharp chemical signature of industrial lubricants and atmospheric processors that have been running for decades without full replacement. Not unpleasant once you're used to it. Belters don't notice it. Inner-system visitors smell it immediately and then stop smelling it within a few hours — which is the moment the station has decided it owns you.
THE SOUND
The centrifuge hum is constant in the Docking Ring — a low-frequency groan felt more than heard, transmitted through the floor and walls and prefab fittings that were never quite properly damped. You stop noticing it the same way you stop noticing your heartbeat. Then something shifts slightly in the rotation balance and the frequency changes a quarter-tone and everyone in the ring notices without knowing why they noticed.
THE LIGHT
In the commercial areas it's warm and deliberate — vendors run their own ambient rigs, and the Coppervein Market has a layered quality from dozens of individual stall setups that creates something almost atmospheric. In the hab corridors it's functional strips that flicker occasionally. In the service level it's industrial, biased toward green from decades of bioluminescent drift. In the hull corridors it's emergency orange and nothing else.
THE SPACE
Prefab panels cover the irregular rock contours, but not all of it. There are gaps. Things grow in the gaps — mostly lichen that the environmental systems technically don't acknowledge exists. The overall effect is of a space made by a machine that didn't care what the result looked like, covered over by humans who did their best and ran out of materials about two thirds of the way through.

◈ HULL MATERIALS — KNOW BEFORE YOU FIRE

REGOLITH ZONES (carved tunnels, hab blocks, the hotel): Walls are meters of asteroid rock. Flechette rounds, stray shots, and even micrometeorites do nothing to structural integrity. Whatever is in the room with you will not be fine, but the station will.

CORPORATE & DOCKING ZONES (exterior corridors, docking ring outer face, prefab modules): Plastisteel and transparisteel composite — rated for vacuum, not for ballistic impact. The pockmarks visible in exterior-facing transparisteel panels are micrometeorite strikes that punched all the way through and were caulked by maintenance droids. A flechette round or misaimed burst at this material in these zones is a breach event.

DOCKING PREFAB SURFACE TREATMENT: The prefab concrete panels throughout the docking ring and exterior corridors are coated in successive layers of water-displacement and vacuum-seal chemicals — a maintenance standard applied every eight months to prevent microcracking from thermal cycling and moisture infiltration. The cumulative effect is a surface that looks nothing like concrete: low-friction, faintly reflective, dark grey shading toward the visual quality of polished titanium. New arrivals routinely assume the docking ring walls are metal. They are not. The coating adds no structural strength — it just looks like it does. In micrograv, the low-friction surface matters: pushing off a coated wall gives less grip than expected. Characters attempting to brace or launch from a coated surface in the hull corridor take an additional –10 to Free Fall checks unless they use the designated anchor points.

PRACTICAL RULE: If the walls are rough and dark, you're in regolith — fire away within reason. If the walls look like polished metal, you're in the docking zone — you're looking at treated concrete and one bad shot from a vacuum incident.

◈ ZONE-BY-ZONE TEXTURE
DOCKING RING — 0.3g
Outermost inhabited ring, closest to the surface, spinning fast enough for about a third of Earth gravity. Enough to keep things on the floor and liquids in cups — but not enough to feel normal. Your gait is wrong. You bounce slightly if you're not careful. Things fall slower than your nervous system expects. SERVICE LEVEL — 0.15g
One ring deeper, spinning slower. Where the infrastructure lives — pipes, conduit, mesh relay nodes, environmental processors, grey water. Movement here is a learned skill. Newcomers either over-commit and float past their destination or under-commit and have to pull themselves back. The connective tissue between the station and its docking struts. Vesta has 0.025g of natural surface pull but treat it as zero for all practical purposes. Tether-and-anchor movement. Sound behaves differently: the structural groans and vibrations of the spinning rings are audible as transmitted resonance through the rock. The exterior always sounds like the station is alive and considering something.

◈ PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS

Vesta is the second-largest body in the Main Asteroid Belt and one of the solar system's few surviving protoplanets — a differentiated world with a rocky mantle and an iron-nickel core, formed in the early solar system before Jupiter's gravity disrupted the Belt's coalescence into a planet. It is not a satellite. Belters notice when you call it one.

Mean diameter: 525 km. Irregular and heavily cratered — not the clean sphere that distance makes it appear.

Surface gravity: 0.025g on the exterior surface. The centrifuge ring generates 0.31g through rotation.

Rotation period: 5.34 hours. Days on Vesta are short. The station runs on a standardised 24-hour cycle regardless, enforced by lighting systems. New arrivals take several days to stop waking at the wrong time.

Albedo: High for a Belt body — Vesta's surface is bright, which made it visible from Earth with the naked eye in antiquity. Miners on the surface work in harsh reflected light.

◈ RHEASILVIA BASIN

The defining geological feature of Vesta is the Rheasilvia impact basin — a crater 505 km across covering most of the southern hemisphere, formed by a collision that nearly shattered the protoplanet. Its central peak rises approximately 22 km from the basin floor, taller than Olympus Mons on Mars. The impact excavated material from Vesta's mantle and scattered it across the inner solar system — a significant fraction of all known meteorites on Earth originated here.

Rheasilvia is not where Vesta Station is built — the polar region provides more stable orientation relative to the sun and better solar power geometry — but it dominates the skyline on any exterior EVA facing south. Miners work its walls. Some of them have never come back up.

◈ VESTA STATION — POPULATION AND GOVERNANCE

Permanent population: Approximately 80,000–120,000. Transient population adds another 20,000–40,000 at any given time — freight crews, commercial visitors, short-term contract workers, egocasters in transit.

Governance: Cooperative council on nominally Extropian principles — decentralised, market-driven, deeply sceptical of inner-system authority. The council is elected by permanent residents. Transient population has no vote, which is a source of low-level tension in commercial zones where transients outnumber residents during peak freight cycles.

Economy: Mining, ore processing, Belt freight transit, commercial egocasting, and a substantial grey market that VSCS monitors with studied indifference. The Coppervein Market is the commercial heart — carved directly into the regolith, expanded organically over four decades, lit by a combination of bioluminescent panels and salvaged industrial lighting that gives the whole district a warm amber cast.

Culture: Vesta-born Belters speak Belter Creole as a first language and treat it as a marker of identity. Anti-inner-system sentiment is endemic and polite — nobody is going to say anything to your face unless you give them a reason, but corp-sec types from Luna or Mars are noticed and resented quietly from the moment they step off the dock.

◈ STATION INFRASTRUCTURE

Vesta Station occupies the northern polar region, carved into the regolith over three generations of expansion. The deepest sections — residential hab blocks, industrial processing, the carved tunnel network — are pure regolith, effectively immune to structural breach. The docking ring and commercial outer zones use plastisteel and transparisteel composite construction, rated for vacuum but not for sustained ballistic impact.

The station has been inhabited continuously since 2041 CE — 63 years, now. The oldest tunnels show it. The newest prefab modules in the docking ring extension show something different: the clean, corporate aesthetic of inner-system construction firms winning contracts the Cooperative would have preferred to give locally.

The station smells of mineral dust, recycled air, and machine oil. It has never smelled like anything else. New arrivals from the inner system find it oppressive. People born here don't notice it at all.

◈ APPENDIX C — COVERT OPERATIONS TOOLS & STEALTHWARE REGULATION
Vesta's regulatory framework was written for an industrial mining station. It handles atmospheric integrity, cargo manifests, and heavy equipment licensing with precision. It has almost nothing coherent about mesh intrusion tools, AR spoofing rigs, or ego-masking software — because when the regulations were drafted, those weren't things dockworkers needed. The result is a patchwork of partial rules, deliberate loopholes, and enforcement gaps that both the PCs and their opponents are exploiting.
◈ WHAT IS ACTUALLY ILLEGAL — VSC-REG-0019
The VSC enforces three specific categories under the existing security code, predating the Audit:
ACTIVE INTRUSION TOOLS
Anything that defeats a mesh lock, spoofs an ID, or intercepts a private transmission while in use is illegal without a VSC security contractor licence. Possession alone is a grey area. Active deployment is not.
EGO-CAPTURE HARDWARE
Stack extraction tools, ego bridge rigs, anything that can fork or copy a mind without consent. Covered by the medical licence requirement, tightened further under VSC-REG-0442. Devraj has one forged medical licence he rents for 10 GP/day.
WEAPONS-GRADE BASILISK HACKS
Flatly illegal. No licence available. VSC treats these identically to kinetic weapons in a pressurised habitat — which is to say, with serious alarm and very little due process.
◈ WHAT IS TECHNICALLY LEGAL
The Extropian bloc on the VCC has defended the following carve-outs for fifteen years:
PASSIVE STEALTHWARE
Mesh anonymisers, traffic scramblers, signal dampeners — fully legal. The ECB sells commercial-grade versions at three kiosks in Coppervein Market. The VSC knows and doesn't care. Half their own officers use them.
TOOLS IN COLD STORAGE
An intrusion suite that isn't running isn't doing anything. VSC enforcement is activity-based, not possession-based. This is a deliberate loophole. Carrying a full offensive toolkit is legal as long as it's dormant.
"EVALUATION COPIES"
ECB-licensed software auditors may carry active intrusion tools under a commercial assessment permit (20 GP, 3 days, ECB). Sable Index almost certainly has one. It's a thin cover but it holds unless someone looks closely.
◈ THE ENFORCEMENT REALITY
The VSC lacks the mesh forensics capability to detect most professional stealthware passively. They can flag anomalies in public mesh logs and investigate after the fact — but real-time detection requires specialist hardware they don't have on the Docking Ring. What they do have is informants: Coppervein vendors who flag unusual purchases, and hotel staff who notice unusual mesh traffic patterns from their nodes.

Petra Vosse noticed MESA's mesh relay burn. She didn't report it. But she noticed. She can be asked about it — and will answer, for the usual price.
The VSC's practical enforcement priority runs roughly: weapons > ego capture > active intrusion > everything else. A sentinel running a mesh anonymiser while conducting surveillance will not be stopped. A sentinel running an active ghost-lock on a commercial unit will be flagged if someone checks the node logs — which under VSC-REG-0442's new 30-day retention window, they now can, up to a month after the fact.

This means past-session actions can have legal consequences in later sessions if Sable Index or Chetverikova pushes the VSC to pull those logs.

◈ APPENDIX F — VESTA STATION PERMIT & BUREAUCRACY GUIDE
Full permit tables, authority breakdown, rep shortcuts, and VSC-REG-0442 operational notes. Three bodies hold overlapping jurisdiction and do not coordinate.
Vesta has no unified permit office. Three bodies hold overlapping jurisdiction and do not coordinate. The practical result is that any permit requiring cross-authority sign-off takes longer than anyone says it will, costs more than anyone quotes, and usually requires knowing someone. Locals navigate it by rep and relationship. Visitors lose days.
VCC
VESTA COOPERATIVE COUNCIL
Habitat access, residential registration, atmospheric zone permits, physical infrastructure. Level 3, mid-ring. Slow. Paper-heavy. Issued VSC-REG-0442.
REP: c-rep / f-rep
ECB
EXTROPIAN COMMERCE BOARD
Business licences, market stall registration, freight manifests, contractor permits. Fast and fee-based. Openly transactional — expedite fees are posted. Sable Index front registered here.
REP: e-rep / g-rep
VSC
VESTA STATION CONSTABULARY
Weapons permits, security contractor licences, stack extraction authorisations, synthmorph registration (post-0442). Docking Ring counter. Understaffed. Half their revenue is permit fees.
REP: c-rep (grudging)

◈ USING REP IN PLACE OF PERMITS
Rep can substitute for, accelerate, or quietly replace most permit processes. The following applies at Vesta under normal conditions — GMs may tighten restrictions if the Audit VSC-REG-0442 is actively being enforced.
c-rep 10–20
Skip queue at VCC or VSC counter. No faster processing — just no wait.
c-rep 30+
Same-day approval on most single-authority permits. Staff expedite without being asked.
e-rep 20+
ECB fast-track on anything commercial. Fee still applies but paperwork evaporates.
g-rep 15+
Freight and docking contacts can push EVA and hull access through without full VCC review.
f-rep 20+
Firewall's shadow network on Vesta can quietly pre-clear commercial cover identities. PCs' cover was handled this way before arrival.
f-rep 30+
Access to VCC internal filing system — enough to check who filed VSC-REG-0442 and whether the redaction is genuine or inserted post-filing.
CROSS-AUTHORITY PERMITS
Anything needing both VCC and VSC sign-off (EVA access, security licences) requires rep or contacts at both bodies separately. One doesn't recognise the other's approvals.
