◈ SITUATION BRIEF

Firewall's response to the Vanth report arrives three days out from Orcus. Not from the usual relay — from a proxy the team has never used, which means someone higher up the sentinel hierarchy has read what was filed and decided this one needs personal handling. The message is short. There is a survey installation on KBO 2119-HR4, registered to Aurochs Deep Mining Consortium, running a piece of proprietary geophysical hardware called a Temporal Isolation Projector. The station went dark eleven days ago. Firewall wants to know why. Routine enough for a first read. The second read is less comfortable: the Temporal Isolation Projector — a TIP unit, in Aurochs' internal documentation — does not appear in any public patent or regulatory filing. Firewall's technology analysis division has seen one reference to it before, in a recovered Consortium black-budget proposal, and flagged it then as a potential existential-class weapon system dressed up as a mining tool. The proposal was shelved. Apparently it wasn't.

KBO 2119-HR4 is a carbonaceous contact-binary object roughly 340 kilometres across — unremarkable, surveyed once in the twenties, subsequently ignored. What makes it of interest to a deep mining operation is its unusual core composition: an anomalously dense metallic interior, rich in platinum-group metals, sitting beneath roughly 80 kilometres of silicate rock and ice. Getting to that core economically requires getting through the mantle without boring through it. The TIP unit does this by projecting a temporal-stasis field across the outer body of the planetoid in a seam pattern — the team's briefing document calls it "stitched like the exterior of a tennis ball" — which arrests all physical and chemical processes within its envelope to an arbitrary depth. The core, not enclosed in the field, continues along its orbital path. Differential movement between the frozen shell and the moving core creates a series of access shafts along the seam boundaries. Elegant. Horrifying. Completely unregulated.

◈ FIREWALL MISSION BRIEF Reach NATAGLACIAL-7, the Aurochs survey station on KBO 2119-HR4. Determine the status of the installation and its 14-person crew. Assess the TIP unit — specifically whether it has been tested at scale, what data it has generated, and whether its specifications could be repurposed for use against inhabited structures. Recover or destroy that data. Do not return the TIP technical documentation to the inner system.
◈ WHAT FIREWALL DOESN'T KNOW The station went dark because something came out of the core. The TIP field cracked open 80 kilometres of silicate rock that had been sealed, isolated, and geologically static for four billion years. Whatever was sealed inside it is no longer inside it. It hatched. It is hungry. The 14-person crew learned this eleven days ago. The team is walking into something that has had eleven days to eat.
◈ THE TIP UNIT — WEAPON POTENTIAL A TIP unit scaled up by a factor of roughly 80 would produce a temporal-stasis envelope large enough to enclose a standard O'Neill cylinder habitat. Applied correctly, it would freeze all biological and mechanical activity within the enclosed volume while leaving the habitat's orbital trajectory unaffected. The people inside would be suspended indefinitely, alive in the moment of freezing, until the field is collapsed — or until it isn't. Aurochs' engineers know this. It is in their own risk assessment, marked as a secondary-use case under the heading "enforcement applications." Firewall knows this is real. The team needs to understand it before they decide what to do with the hardware.
◈ THE SILICON PREDATOR — WHAT IT IS

The scientific community does not have a name for it because, until eleven days ago, the scientific community did not know it existed. The Aurochs survey team has twelve pages of increasingly fragmented notes, the last of which ends mid-sentence. What follows is reconstructed from those notes and from what the team will be able to observe directly.

LITHOHEX — SILICON PREDATOR
XENOBIOLOGICAL ENTITY · SUBSURFACE ORIGIN · MINERAL-METABOLIC LIFECYCLE · NON-SAPIENT / HIGHLY REACTIVE
SPEED: Very Fast (microgravity, 6-legged locomotion)
SIZE: 0.4–0.8m body, legs up to 1.5m
SENSES: EM field detection (primarily), vibration, heat
ARMOUR: Silicate shell — equivalent to light combat plate
ATTACK: Mandible clamp (anchors to substrate/target) + acid-flux secretion
WEAKNESS: Extreme cold, EMP disrupts EM sensing, no infil vs non-silicon

SILICON METABOLISM: The Lithohex cannot digest organics. It processes silicon-based compounds — silicate rock, semiconductor substrates, glass, ceramic composites. It digests by secreting a highly acidic flux compound onto the target surface, dissolving the silicon matrix, and absorbing the resulting silicate slurry through a ventral feeding structure. The process takes 8–15 minutes for a standard electronics module, 3–6 hours for a structural panel. It does not eat people. It eats the electronics around people, and it does not stop if a person is in the way.

WASTE PRODUCT: The metabolic byproduct of silicon digestion is a high-temperature vitrified globule — a small, irregular glass bead, ranging from marble-sized to roughly fist-sized, deposited wherever the creature has fed. These globules are scattered throughout every space a Lithohex has occupied. They are sharp. They are everywhere. They are the first thing the team sees when they enter a compromised module.

REPRODUCTION: The initial hatching produced a single creature from a dormant spore-case the size of a large boulder. The spore-case has since fractured into secondary dormant units, approximately 8–12 of which the team will find distributed throughout the station, in warm enclosed spaces near active electronics. Secondary hatching is triggered by the EM emissions of active hardware — specifically by semiconductor switching frequencies. If the team powers up station systems, things will hatch. If they leave them cold, the secondary spores remain dormant. This is the session's central dilemma.

SOCIAL BEHAVIOUR: Not sapient. Does not communicate. Does not coordinate. Does not feel threatened by anything except extreme cold and electromagnetic disruption. It investigates active electronics the way a moth investigates a light source: compulsively, persistently, and without apparent concept of danger. Multiple individuals do not cooperate — they compete for the same food source, which can be useful. Two Lithohex in the same module will fight over the same terminal before they notice the team.

DETECTION: The EM-sensing organs are lateral and face-mounted. The creature can detect operating electronics at up to 40 metres through standard bulkhead material. Powered-down equipment produces no useful signal. Personnel in synthmorphs or with active muse-hardware register as a faint signal — enough to attract attention if the creature is within roughly 10 metres and there is no more compelling source nearby.

The Lithohex is not a monster in the horror-movie sense. It is an animal doing what animals do. It happens to be an animal that treats the entire technological substrate of transhumanity as food. The dread comes not from malice but from indifference — the session's creatures are not trying to harm anyone. They just don't distinguish between a circuit board and a person attached to one.
◈ NATAGLACIAL-7 — STATION LAYOUT & STATUS

NATAGLACIAL-7 is a modular deep-survey installation: six pressurised habitat rings connected by access corridors, anchored to the surface of KBO 2119-HR4 by deep-bore pitons. From a distance it is unremarkable — any outer-system survey station looks roughly like this. Up close, something is wrong with its surface. The exterior panels are covered in protrusions: irregular, glassy, catching the distant sun and throwing back cold light. Not ice. Too smooth, too rounded, too warm. The glass globules have been deposited even on the hull exterior, through ventilation gaps and external cable runs. The station looks like it has grown spines.

Module A — Docking Ring
FIRST CONTACT GLOBULE EVIDENCE ENVIRONMENT

The docking ring is still pressurised. The airlock cycles correctly. The first thing the team sees when they step inside is the floor — glass everywhere. Small rounded beads, larger irregular lumps, the odd piece still showing a translucent skin where it hasn't fully vitrified, all catching the suit lamps in tiny cold flashes. They crunch underfoot in microgravity — except in microgravity they don't crunch, they skitter, tumbling in slow arcs, filling the air with a faint crystalline clatter when anyone moves. The walls tell the story: the control panels along the docking ring have been eaten. Not destroyed — dissolved. The screens, the bezels, the sensor arrays. Clean circular damage where the creature set its body down and fed, surrounding each panel in a dry glass moat. The structural alloys are intact. The semiconductor internals are gone.

The docking ring currently has no active creature. But one passed through recently — within the last 18 hours, based on the freshness of some of the glass deposits. The creature is in Module C or Module E (Science Ops or TIP Hub). The team can map its path by following the globule trail.

Module E — TIP Control Hub
PRIMARY NEST FIELD CONTROLS EXTREME HAZARD WEAPON INTEL

Module E is the hottest space in the station — not because of heat in the conventional sense, but because the TIP unit's power draw is enormous and the module is warm, full of running electronics, and the creature knows it. The primary Lithohex has established something that might charitably be called a nest here: it returns to Module E between feeding excursions, and has been depositing glass waste continuously for eleven days. The entire module is encased in glass. The walls, the ceiling, the TIP control consoles, the structural struts — all of it coated in a dense glass matrix that has hardened into a crystalline lattice, partially sealing the module into a kind of organic glass-cathedral. It is beautiful in a way that is deeply, completely wrong.

The creature is present in Module E. It will not leave voluntarily while the TIP unit is running — the power draw is too attractive. The team cannot operate the TIP controls without getting past it. There are several approaches:

◈ APPROACH: DRAW IT OUT If the team powers up a system elsewhere in the station — Science Ops, Habitation, the primary comms relay — the creature will be drawn toward the new EM source. This clears Module E for roughly 8–12 minutes. It also begins hatching any dormant spore cases near the powered-up system. The team gets access to the TIP controls at the cost of new active creatures elsewhere.
◈ APPROACH: FIGHT IT The primary creature can be killed, but not easily. Its silicate armour is resistant to kinetic weapons; it is vulnerable to shaped-charge demolition charges (Yusuf has two), cryogenic agent (the station's fire suppression has residual cryo-foam in Module F), or EMP (disrupts its sensing organs, causes disorientation for ~30 seconds, and it will flail destructively in that period). A direct fight in an enclosed glass-shard environment is bad for everyone. The team will take cuts and impact damage just from the glass the creature dislodges.
◈ APPROACH: USE THE FIELD AGAINST IT The TIP field boundary runs through the access shaft in Module F. A creature lured into the shaft and pushed past the temporal boundary will be frozen in the field. It won't be dead — just stopped, suspended in the moment of crossing the threshold. This is elegant and deeply strange and MESA will note that it is also a small demonstration of exactly what the weapon version of this technology would do to a habitat full of people. The team should feel that parallel.
Regardless of how they deal with the creature, the team needs to make a decision about the TIP unit: shut it down, or leave it running. Shutting it down collapses the temporal field — the access shafts close, the mining operation ends, and any creature currently inside the field boundary is instantly returned to normal time. It also destroys the only working TIP hardware in existence. Leaving it running preserves the technology for study but also means whatever is still inside the core access shafts remains accessible — and the team doesn't know what else might be dormant down there.
◈ TIP UNIT — TECHNICAL DATA (IF RECOVERED)
The control console, once the creature is dealt with, contains the full Aurochs engineering specification. It confirms everything Ilara said. The Phase Two projector design is 80× the current scale, mounting on a high-delta-V platform, powered by two clustered fusion reactors. Field projection range: up to 800 metres from the platform. Projected coverage diameter: 2.2 kilometres — sufficient to enclose a standard cylinder or torus habitat. Estimated freeze depth: unlimited (full enclosure). The field is designed to be invisible to standard sensor sweeps until activated. The project codename is AMBER LATTICE. Two Consortium corporate divisions have approved Phase Two funding. Construction has not yet begun. The team is holding the only extant copy of this specification. What they do with it is the session's final question.
Module F — The Deep Bore Shaft
INSIDE THE FIELD STRANGE PHYSICS OPTIONAL / EXPLORATORY

No one has been down the shaft since the creature came up it. The TIP field boundary is at approximately 40 metres depth — marked by a faint shimmer across the shaft diameter, the same heat-haze distortion the team saw from orbit. Above it: normal time, the station, the creature, the mess. Below it: stopped time. The shaft walls below the boundary show the rock in the exact moment the TIP field engaged — a millimetre-thin layer of loose silicate dust frozen in mid-drift. A tiny crack in the rock wall at 55 metres, crossed by the boundary, shows the crack stopped on one side and propagating normally on the other. Everything below the boundary is four billion years old in the moment it's stopped in.

A person crossing the temporal boundary does not stop — the field only arrests matter in place relative to the surrounding geological substrate. Moving bodies passing through the boundary are excluded from the stasis effect. This is why the mining access works: equipment can travel in and out. A person descending the shaft below the boundary enters a place where time, for the rock around them, has stopped, but they themselves continue normally.

This scene is optional but offers a profoundly strange experience. Below the boundary, the team is inside stopped time. The silence is absolute — no vibration, no geological micro-tremors, no thermal creep in the rock. The shaft walls feel carved from glass. At 70 metres depth, the void cavity where the spore-case was found is still open, and the original case — the large boulder-sized primary — is still there, cracked open, the hinge of the hatch still frozen in the moment of opening. Whatever came out of it walked upward through the TIP boundary and into normal time. Whatever else might be in the secondary cavities deeper in the core has not been surveyed.
MESA — ON CROSSING THE BOUNDARY "I am processing this very carefully. I am in a place where time has stopped, and I am the only thing moving. I have — some feelings about this that I am not going to describe at length because we are in a crisis. But I want to note that this is the strangest thing I have experienced in this body, and that is a meaningful statement given everything." [Pause.] "The void cavity at 70 metres shows secondary chamber formations. At least three additional chambers, sealed. I cannot determine if they are also occupied. We have no way of knowing without opening them." [Longer pause.] "I think we should not open them."
MESA is right. There are secondary chambers. The team may discover signs of them — regular ovoid shapes in the cavity wall, the same dimensions as the primary case — but the session's scope does not require opening them. The implication is enough. The system may have been seeded with multiple specimens. The TIP device has opened access to all of them. Shutting it down re-seals the shaft. Leaving it on doesn't.
The Decision — What to Do With AMBER LATTICE
EXISTENTIAL CHOICE MESA WEAPON THREAT ARC CONSEQUENCE

Before anyone leaves the station, the team has to decide what to do with three things: the TIP unit itself, the engineering data, and the creatures. These are not abstract policy questions. They are standing in Module E with a Firewall mission brief that says "recover or destroy" the data, survivors who deserve to leave, and MESA who has an opinion she has been composing since she first saw the field boundary from orbit. This is the session's final scene and it should be run as a real conversation, not a skill roll.

◈ OPTION: DESTROY THE TIP UNIT Yusuf has the demolition charges. Destroying the control hub collapses the field, reseals the shaft, and eliminates the only working prototype — along with its engineering logs if those haven't already been copied. Aurochs loses their proof of concept. Phase Two is set back by years, possibly permanently. The silicon predators, if any remain in the field-enclosed zone, are re-sealed below the closed shaft. What's already loose in the station is still a problem — but manageable.
◈ OPTION: PRESERVE THE TIP UNIT Leave it running. File a report. Let Firewall or another body decide what to do with a working temporal-isolation projector sitting on a rock at 52 AU. The creatures in the station still have to be dealt with. The secondary chambers below the boundary remain accessible. Someone will come back here. Firewall, Ozma, Aurochs, someone. The question is who arrives first.
◈ OPTION: COPY THE DATA AND DESTROY THE HARDWARE The team retains the full AMBER LATTICE specification. They have evidence of a weapons program. They have leverage, if they choose to use it, over Aurochs. They have the technical specification for something that could freeze a habitat. They are now the only people in the solar system with that specification, besides whatever Aurochs has on their secured servers. This is the most dangerous outcome from a Firewall perspective — and the one that most resembles how the MERIDIAN-PRIME thread has been accumulating, session by session, in this team's hands.
MESA — HER POSITION, STATED ONCE "I've been thinking about the report we filed at Vanth. About what we said and what we omitted and why. I want to say something clearly, which is that I think the pattern we've been running — hold the dangerous thing, decide later, carry it ourselves — has a cost that we haven't fully accounted for yet. Every time we keep the thing, we become a little more responsible for what it does next." [Long pause.] "I'm not telling you to destroy it. I'm telling you that I understand if you do. And I'm telling you that if you keep it, I'd like us to talk about why, specifically. Not 'it might be useful.' Why. What we actually intend to do with a weapon that stops time." [Quiet.] "I'll support whatever you decide. I wanted you to make the decision knowing that I've been thinking about it."
The AMBER LATTICE decision is designed to echo everything the campaign has asked about authority, knowledge, and responsibility since Vesta. The team has been accumulating powerful and dangerous information since Session 03. This is the question the campaign has been building toward: at what point does holding the thing make you the threat? MESA's framing is the correct one. Let the players sit with it.
CONSEQUENCE PREVIEW: If the team destroys the hardware and data — Aurochs' Phase Two program faces a significant setback; Firewall is grateful; the team gains rep and resources for the final arc. If they copy the data — they now hold AMBER LATTICE; this information will matter in the final arc in ways that depend on who the team shares it with. If they do nothing and report the situation — Firewall sends a recovery team; Aurochs corporate security arrives; the station becomes a contested site. Any of these are valid. The final arc accommodates all of them.
Departure — The Specimen Question
MESA XENOBIOLOGY ETHICAL OPTIONAL

At some point before the team boards the Perennial, MESA raises something she has been holding since Module C. She does not frame it as a request. She frames it as a question, because she genuinely does not know the answer and wants the team to think with her. The Lithohex is, as far as anyone in the solar system knows, the only documented non-terrestrial life form with a verifiable endogenous biology — not a TITAN construct, not a xenomorph artifact, not a simulation. Something that evolved on its own, billions of years ago, in a sealed rock, 52 AU from the sun. It is also an active threat to every semiconductor-dependent system in transhumanity. Both of these things are true. MESA is not asking to take one home. She is asking what the team thinks should happen to it — and whether, if they destroy the station, they are comfortable with what that means.

MESA "I've been trying to think about this the right way. The creature in Module E — the original — came out of 80 kilometres of rock it's been sealed in for four billion years. It didn't come here. We opened the rock. We cracked the seal. We brought the technology that woke it up." [Pause.] "I'm not saying it's our fault eleven people died. I'm saying that the creature did not choose to be here and it is not doing anything other than what it evolved to do and that those facts don't make it safe but they do make it — something other than a monster." [Longer pause.] "Ilara wants to document it. She asked me privately. She wants to write the species description. She's been composing it for nine days. I think she should be allowed to do that." [Very quiet.] "And then I think we need to talk about whether we leave it alive."
There is no correct answer to this question. MESA is not lobbying for a decision — she is doing what she has done since Session 03: helping the team think carefully about things they might otherwise act on quickly. If the team decides to destroy the Lithohex and the spore cases — that is defensible and probably wise. If the team decides to seal the station and report the organism's existence for supervised scientific study — that is also defensible and creates a complication. If the team takes a non-viable tissue sample for Ilara to analyze — that is possible and MESA will facilitate it. What matters is that the decision is made consciously, with the team understanding that they are making a choice about a living thing that has done nothing wrong to them as a species, only to transhumanity's tools.
ILARA — IF ASKED "I know what you're going to decide. I'd just like to take a tissue sample first, if that's possible. I have the collection equipment in Module D's storage. Whatever happens after — at least it existed somewhere other than eleven pages of notes that nobody outside this room will ever read." [She looks at the wall.] "Its name — if you're going to let me name it — is Lithovorus silicophagus. Silicon-eater. I'd like it to be named."
◈ GM NOTES — RUNNING THIS SESSION

This session runs on two registers simultaneously. On the surface it is a survival horror scenario with a classic structure: trapped station, active predator, limited resources, clock. Below the surface it is asking the campaign's central question in its most concrete form yet: what do you do with a technology that could freeze an O'Neill cylinder full of people, and who gets to make that decision? The creature is not an obstacle to that question — it is the demonstration model. The thing that hatched when the rock was opened is the living proof of what happens when you crack something sealed. AMBER LATTICE does the same thing to habitats that the TIP field did to the silicate layer: it opens the rock, and you don't know what's inside until the seal is broken.

The Lithohex should never be played as a slasher-movie monster. It is an animal. It is indifferent, not malicious. The horror comes from its scale of effect — not the individual encounter, but what it means that it exists. If the session does its job, the table will feel the strangeness of hating a creature that has done nothing wrong by its own nature. That discomfort is the session's gift to the final arc.

The TIP field is the session's most important element for the arc. The team will understand — viscerally, from standing inside it — what this technology can do. Abstract geopolitical threat becomes physical experience: you crossed a boundary, time stopped around you, you kept moving, you understood. When the AMBER LATTICE specification describes doing this to a habitat, the team will not need an explanation of why that is unacceptable. They were inside one.

MESA has been carrying the weight of the campaign's accumulated knowledge since Vesta. This session gives her the space to say, plainly, what she thinks about the pattern of decisions the team has made. Not an accusation — an observation. She is the one who logs the mission reports, maintains the threat files, and remembers every omission from every Firewall transmission. She is also the one who named a xenobiological organism silicon-eater in her internal notes at 3 AM in a dead science station. Both of these things are MESA. Use them both.

The session has two clocks: the survivors' atmosphere supply (8–12 hours), and the spore hatching mechanic (triggered by powered systems). Neither clock should be secret — Ilara knows her atmosphere situation and will tell the team, and the team will learn the spore hatching mechanic from Ilara's briefing in Scene 3. Informed pressure is better than hidden pressure. The players should feel the urgency, not discover they were underwater.

Spore hatchings are the escalation tool. Start with none active beyond the primary creature. Each time the team powers up a system, one or more hatch. By the midpoint, there should be 2–3 active Lithohex in the station. By the final push to Module E, the station should feel genuinely dangerous. But never more creatures than the team can plausibly manage — the goal is pressure, not TPK.

The session ends when the team, the survivors, and whatever data or samples they've chosen to carry are aboard the Perennial and the station is behind them — destroyed, sealed, or left for someone else to deal with. That last act of looking back at the station (now dark, or burning, or still running) is the session's final image. Give it a moment.

Whatever the team does with AMBER LATTICE becomes the central complication of the final arc. A weapon that can freeze habitats is a threat to every populated structure in the solar system, and the team is now the only people who have encountered the prototype and understand its full implications. Whether they destroy it, report it, or carry it, the consequences shape the final arc's opening condition.

Dahl, if the team accepted his arrangement at Vanth, receives a message three days after departure: Ozma has flagged KBO 2119-HR4 as a point of interest. They don't know about the Lithohex. They know about the TIP technology. Dahl's message to the team is two words: "How bad?"

MESA's question about the Lithohex — the specimen, the name, the existence-outside-a-dead-station question — is not resolved here. Ilara's tissue sample, if taken, travels with the team. So does the question of whether Lithovorus silicophagus gets to exist in the scientific record, and under what circumstances, and whether Aurochs' deep bore operation will eventually open another shaft, somewhere else, into another sealed cavity, and whether anyone will be watching.

◈ THE SEMAPHORE — WHAT SHE WAS POINTING AT
Sauelsuesor currently has four limbs extended into the outer system. The team has now been to the locations corresponding to three of them: Gutenberg Station, FK7-class Kuiper coordinates, Arrokoth. The fourth arm has been pointing here — KBO 2119-HR4 — for longer than any of the others. Outer-system observer logs date its first appearance to nineteen months before the team's arrival. She was pointing at the Lithohex before it hatched.
THE IMPLICATION: The Lithohex is not an accident. It is not bad luck that the research team cracked the rock and the predator emerged. Something that can see across the solar system from near the sun saw this object nineteen months ago and knew what was inside it. The Semaphore cannot intervene. She cannot travel. She cannot speak to anyone with sufficient authority or reach to act. All she can do is point. She has been pointing at this specific piece of frozen rock for a year and a half, and the first people to get close enough to understand why just docked.
MESA — ON ARRIVAL
"The fourth arm. This is it." She does not elaborate immediately. She processes the hull of KBO 2119-HR4 on optical scan, the glass spikes, the emergency beacon. Then: "She saw it before it was dangerous. Before the rock was opened. She was pointing at what it would become. I think she knew it would hatch. I think she's been trying to get someone here before that happened for nineteen months." A long pause. "We're late."
KNOW: ASTROBIOLOGY / KNOW: TITAN TECH
The Semaphore's detection method is unknown, but her track record across the campaign suggests sensitivity to potential X-risks at extreme range — things that are not yet dangerous but will become so. The Lithohex as it existed inside the KBO was dormant, inert, and completely undetectable by any transhumanist instrument. She pointed at it anyway. Whatever she senses, it is not a current threat signature. It is a future one.
The Temporal Isolation Protocol unit is the campaign's final convergence point. The Semaphore has been pointing at this location for nineteen months. She cannot act — she can only indicate. The team is the action she pointed toward. The TIP unit is the tool. This is the scenario she was hoping for when she extended that arm.
FOR THE GM: You do not need to make this explicit. The players may put it together themselves — or they may not until after the session, in debrief, when someone says "wait, was the whole campaign her trying to get us out here in time?" That realisation landing late is better than you explaining it. The thread was always there. They followed it. They were the warning she sent.
If MESA is present for the TIP decision and the team understands the Semaphore thread: "Whatever happens here — she saw it. She's been watching. I find I want to get this right. For her sake, as much as ours."
