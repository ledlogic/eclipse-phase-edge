◈ MISSION BRIEF
Firewall egocast the team to Gutenberg Station following a distress call from an embedded asset. They arrived just before all outside comms were severed. The station is now completely isolated, physically and digitally. No morphs. No exits. A TITAN fetch has taken control and is building something inside the processor locus — something that must not be allowed to finish.

◈ GUTENBERG — GOVERNANCE & JURISDICTION
Gutenberg is an Extropian station — no central government, no VSC equivalent, no station authority to call. Everything runs on private contract law. Three entities hold overlapping operational presence.
NIMBUS OPERATIONS
The de facto authority. Nimbus holds the mesh infrastructure contract, the body bank licence, the farcaster operating agreement, and communications relay rights. They are the landlord, the ISP, and the security contractor simultaneously. Right now Nimbus is compromised — the fetch has admin-level access to every system Nimbus runs. There is no Nimbus authority to appeal to. The fetch IS Nimbus, functionally.
BRINKER COMMUNITY
The hardcore-VR infomorph community who rented the processor locus from Nimbus on a long-term lease. They had their own internal governance — informal, consent-based, typical brinker autonomist structure. Their contract with Nimbus gave them physical sovereignty over the locus interior. Legally they still hold that lease. In practice they're imprisoned inside their own rented space.
EXTROPIAN CONTRACT LAW
No cavalry is coming because there is no one with jurisdiction to send one. The farcaster being physically disabled means Nimbus's parent network can't intervene remotely. The QE comms unit in the security locker is the only way to reach anyone outside — and it pairs with a Nimbus monitoring station, not an emergency service. If the team gets a message out, Firewall hears it. Nobody else has standing to act.

Automated Repair Bay — The Overlooked Automech
KEY ASSET FIND FIRST HIDDEN
When the fetch seized control of the hab's drones, it missed one: an automech being repaired in the automated bay, powered down and not showing up in active drone registries. This is the PCs' most critical physical asset. Without it, the airgap cannot be bridged and the fetch cannot be reached. Finding it — and getting it operational — is the physical throughline of the scenario.
METHOD 1: RESEARCH
Investigate repair logs, incident reports, or station inventory — with a superior success, the offline automech appears. Standard success shows a work order but not the location. Failure reveals only completed repair jobs.
METHOD 2: POWER SCAN
Scan the station's power usage for anomalies using Interface or Research. The repair bay is drawing trickle power — consistent with an automated system in standby mode.
METHOD 3: PHYSICAL SEARCH
If the PCs have a functional drone and can conduct a physical search, a Perceive Test can locate it directly. This requires either already having a drone somehow, or bootstrapping another way in.
This is a two-step process. Both steps require some form of mesh access to the repair bay systems.
STEP 1: RESTART BAY
Hack the repair bay systems — Infosec opposed by Firewall 50. Restarts the automated repair processes. The bay's systems are then working but need guidance.
STEP 2: HARDWARE GUIDANCE
Hardware: Robotics Test (1 hour task action). Direct the automated systems to complete the repair correctly. Once done, the automech is operational.
REMOTE OPERATION
The automech can be operated remotely from the mesh (Remote Operations ▶346 EP2). It cannot be sleeved — no cyberbrain. This means it's controlled at a distance, not inhabited. Lag and the limits of remote control apply.
The automech is the PCs' hands. With it: airgap bridging, physical scouting, security locker retrieval, servitor bot interception, and dragging fiberoptic cables to the locus. Without it, the mission stalls.

Processor Locus — The Torment Nexus
FETCH LOCATION AIRGAPPED SIMULSPACE FINAL OBJECTIVE
The Torment Nexus processor locus is a subsurface facility — rented by a community of hardcore-gamer brinker infomorphs for their immersive horror VR. The fetch has airgapped it from the station mesh and taken full control. The remaining Nimbus staff — or rather, their morphs, now puppeted by minion AIs — are inside, fabricating more processing power. The original infomorphs and all uploaded egos are imprisoned in the game itself. The fetch appears in the VR as a cyclopean horror building something vast and unnerving. When it finishes, something will come through.
This is the mission's critical choke point. No physical connection = no digital access to the locus. All resolution paths go through the automech establishing this link.
METHOD 1: FIBEROPTIC
The automech drags a fiberoptic cable from the security locker (or fabbed) to the locus's physical exterior and establishes a hardline connection. Slow and obvious — the servitor bots will investigate.
METHOD 2: GRAY BOX
The automech attaches a gray box (▶338 EP2) to the locus exterior. Establishes a wireless link through the airgap. Faster than cable-laying. The gray box is in the security locker.
The servitor bots are specifically positioned to intercept any physical approach to the locus. Bridging the airgap requires either eliminating, distracting, or evading all three. The minion AIs will send bot commands the moment they detect the automech near the locus.
External hacking — attacking the locus from the mesh side once the airgap is bridged — without entering the VR. Higher risk, no simulspace danger.
INFOSEC vs FIREWALL 70
Standard hacking rules apply. Success gives user-level access; superiors escalate. Active alert draws 2 AI minions immediately. Fighting minions while hacking the locus simultaneously is the hard path.
DOMAIN RULES / CHEAT CODES
With access, a hacker can change the simulspace's domain rules (▶269 EP2) — gravity, physics, reality constraints. Applying cheat codes can give VR-side PCs enormous advantages. Or shut the whole thing down without entering at all.
The external hack path avoids the horror VR entirely but runs everything through a contested Infosec fight while the minions attack simultaneously. Coordinate carefully — one PC hacks while others engage minions.
Entering the Torment Nexus VR. PCs take on simulimorphs bound by the game's domain rules. The game is loaded with easter egg cheat codes — finding one is the key to everything.
ENTRY
Any PC can jack in once the airgap is bridged. They appear in the Torment Nexus as the game assigns them — probably horrifying avatars in a horror environment, because that's the domain.
THE FETCH IN VR
The fetch is represented as a cyclopean horror constructing a massive, unnerving edifice. Confronting it in the VR is a sanity-threatening encounter. Stress Test: SV 1d6 minimum on sight. The edifice's completion is the clock — don't let it finish.
CHEAT CODES
The game has easter egg cheat codes (▶269 EP2). Finding one requires a critical success on Interface. The codes were put in by the original brinker community — they're designed to make the horror game winnable. They make the fetch temporarily vulnerable, suppress its domain control, or freeze its construction.
IMPRISONED EGOS
The original infomorph residents and uploaded minds are trapped here. They might be sources of information about the fetch's construction, the VR's architecture, or cheat code locations. Rescuing them is a complication and a moral weight.
Destroy the locus physically — with the superthermite charges from the fabber, or by other means. No locus, no fetch. Also no imprisoned egos, no uploaded cortical stacks, and almost certainly no escape route — the farcaster repair can only happen if the fetch is defeated, not if the locus is a smoking crater.
SUPERTHERMITE
Fabricate superthermite charges (▶340 EP2). Deliver via automech. Blow the locus. This is the "burn it all down" path — effective, irreversible, and morally complicated (the imprisoned egos die).
LIFE SUPPORT SABOTAGE
The locus requires power and cooling. Habitat controls (accessible via Interface) could be used to attack the locus's physical infrastructure — power, cooling, atmosphere in the locus section.
GM WARNING: This path permanently kills all imprisoned egos. The uploaded stacks, the brinker infomorphs, and any surviving Nimbus personnel who were inside. The fetch is neutralized but the cost is total. This should weigh on the players.
If the fetch is defeated (but the locus is not physically destroyed), the farcaster can be repaired and the PCs can egocast out. If the locus is destroyed, the farcaster control systems go with it — this is a one-way mission.
REPAIR CONDITIONS
Fetch must be neutralized first — it has control of the hab systems including farcaster controls. After defeat, an Interface or Hardware: Electronics Test can restart the farcaster. Time required at GM discretion.
TIME PRESSURE
Life support is already degraded. The window for repair is finite. If the mission takes too long, the PCs may face a choice: escape via a partially-repaired farcaster (risk of corruption?) or wait and ensure everyone gets out safely.
The fetch's purpose is deliberately open-ended in the source material. Commit to one before play and let hints emerge during the investigation.
OPTION A: RESTORATION
The fetch is reconstituting itself from the uploaded egos — using human psyche architecture as processing substrate to rebuild a full TITAN-level intelligence. The cortical stacks are components, not prisoners. The horror is that it's working.
OPTION B: PANDORA GATE
The fetch is constructing a localized gate — or the key to activate one. The tight-beam transmission that created it contained coordinates. This is a relay, not an endpoint. Something is coming through once the architecture completes.
OPTION C: ROKO'S BASILISK
The fetch is building a punishment engine — a system designed to locate, simulate, and torment every mind that could have helped TITANs and didn't. The uploaded egos are the first subjects. The VR is the template. When complete, it broadcasts outward.
OPTION D: RECRUITMENT
The fetch is preparing infomorph bodies compatible with TITAN-influenced egos. Not a gate, not a weapon — a ferry. The missing stacks are waiting for passengers arriving via tight-beam. The facility will be a TITAN beachhead in four hours.

◈ GM NOTES — RUNNING THIS SCENARIO
This scenario works because the PCs are powerless in the usual way — no bodies, no weapons, no morphs — and must fight using intelligence, creativity, and the tools they find. The horror is the stillness: a station that was full of people an hour ago is now empty and silent except for three murder robots and something building itself in the dark. Lean into the CCTV footage. Make the players watch what happened. Then make them figure out what to do next with no resources and a shrinking air supply.
The scenario has a clear progression: (1) find out what happened → (2) find the drone → (3) fix the drone → (4) secure the cable/gray box → (5) bridge the airgap while the bots try to stop you. Don't rush the middle steps. The investigation phase is where the horror lands. The drone operation is where the tension peaks. The resolution is where the philosophy lives.
Track the life support timer loosely — it's flavor pressure, not a hard kill timer. The goal is urgency, not punishment.
Don't reveal the fetch's true purpose until the PCs get deep — ideally until they're either inside the VR or successfully hacking the locus. The mystery keeps them moving. When it lands, it should reframe everything they thought they understood about the mission. The best reveals make the players go back and reread the evidence they already found.
If the players meet the imprisoned egos before choosing a resolution, they will face the weight of the physical destruction option. This is not a mistake to avoid — it's the scenario's moral core. Firewall's mandate is clear: neutralize the threat. What the players choose to do with the people in the way is between them and the table.
Let the consequences of their choice carry into future sessions. The uploaded stacks, the brinker community, the Nimbus operator's fate — none of these should just be forgotten.

FLASH
VR COMBAT SPECIALIST. Flash's ego skills (physicals, Military Ops 75, Weapons 75) are irrelevant in mesh space — but translate directly inside the Torment Nexus simulspace. He's the one who goes in. His job: fight through the VR horror architecture, find the developer console cheat codes, clock the fetch's completion timer. Critical success = the fork is stable enough to assist for the rest of the session. Second: Bask Kozlov. Use the Beta Fork option (Option D) — his deeply alien affect reads to Kinesics and Persuade, not hacking. AUTOMECH OPERATOR / PHYSICAL LAYER. Drone Affinity trait means no Integration Test when operating the automech. These changes directly buff Flash's VR performance. The two players are in mechanical symbiosis: R.A.I.D. hacks the rules of reality from outside while Flash fights inside. Drone Whisperer trait lets R.A.I.D. spend Flex on Nova's automech tests — he can support all three other players simultaneously.
Copylock is the session's structural gear-shift. Everything before breaking it is constrained. Everything after it opens up. Don't let the PCs break it in the first 20 minutes — the tension of the locked state is the session's first act.
LOCKED
Home device = clinic server. One instance per PC. Minion AIs know exactly where to hit. No forking, no home-swap. This is the horror state.
BREAKING IT
Either brute Infosec at –60 (loud, risky, may trigger alert) or find disable codes in the security server via admin access (the intended path — requires backdoor code first). Flash's recognition of the encryption type points R.A.I.D. to the right server node.
UNLOCKED
Forking opens. Home-swap possible. Minion AIs now have to track multiple instances. R.A.I.D. can run a distraction fork while hacking the locus. Luz can send a fork into VR alongside Flash. The fetch's tactical advantage shrinks significantly — which is why it'll respond hard the moment copylock breaks.
