◈ MERIDIAN-ECHO-2 — THE TITAN-KILLER THREAD
This thread should surface late in the session — after the identification string, not before. It is not confirmed. It is the shape of something the team has to decide whether to pursue. Do not present it as revelation; present it as the horrifying implication of evidence they already hold.
◈ WHAT THE EVIDENCE SUGGESTS
TRAINER-INSTANCE — SIMULATION LOAD
The relay log activity pattern shows MERIDIAN-ECHO-2 running dense simulation bursts on an escalating schedule. Early in the logs: long quiet periods, short bursts. Recent logs: bursts nearly continuous, quiet periods shrinking. Something is being trained. The training load is approaching the upper bound of what any non-TITAN system should be able to generate as adversarial challenge. Whatever is being trained against needs to be harder every cycle or it stops learning.
KNOW: TITAN TECH / RESEARCH 60
The only known application for this kind of escalating adversarial simulation architecture — where the challenge generator must keep pace with the subject's improving capability — is training a system to defeat an opponent that continuously adapts. The implicit endpoint: the training program must eventually simulate a near-TITAN-level threat to keep the subject challenged. It may already be doing so. The subject of that training is unknown from this data alone.
THE AMBIGUITY — FORK OR DERIVATIVE?
The cognitive fingerprint in the relay logs cuts both ways. Evidence for fork: the compression artifact is MESA's undocumented quirk, impossible to replicate without access to her actual processing architecture. Evidence against: MESA has no memory gap consistent with a covert fork — unless it happened during Iapetus storage, the Gutenberg digital operation, or any other period she was running on hardware she didn't fully control. A derivative built from stolen cognitive models could approximate the fingerprint without being the real thing. MESA does not know which is true. The team cannot determine it from this data.
◈ WHAT MESA SAYS IF PUSHED TO THE EDGE OF HER KNOWLEDGE
MESA — FINAL ADMISSION "Someone is building something that can kill TITANs. They built the trainer that teaches it from my architecture — or from me directly, I don't know which. They have been running it for 847 days. It is close to ready." [Pause.] "I don't know if that's the most dangerous thing I've ever learned or the most important. I've been trying to decide since the interface and I still don't know."
◈ QUESTIONS THIS THREAD OPENS — DO NOT ANSWER YET
Who built it and why — Firewall? Ozma? Neither? Someone the campaign hasn't named yet.
What is the subject being trained — a weapons system, an AI, something biological, something else entirely.
Why does the trainer need to be located here, at the end of a beacon pointing at something outside the solar system. What does the signal source have to do with the training program.
Whether MESA-2 is aware of MESA-1 — and what it would mean if it is. Whether MESA wants to find it. Whether she has a right to shut it down if she does.
◈ GM NOTES — RUNNING THIS SESSION
This session should feel different from everything before it. The team has been in corridors and stations and other people's problems. Now they're in the outer dark with something that predates the TITANs and their own morphs on their backs. Lead with the scale. Give the PCs the view from the GS-Perennial's port window — the sun is a bright star, indistinguishable from many others, and they are very far from it.
The wonder comes first. Then the signal stops. Then the silence. The dread is in what MESA says in that silence — and what she doesn't.
MESA's hypothesis — that the signal was a coordinate, not a call for help and not a weapon — should come out in stages. The full picture: the signal is a marker left by a pre-TITAN intelligence for something that was supposed to come after them. The TITANs found it. The TITANs tried to follow the coordinate. The Fall may have interrupted that journey — or completed it.
MESA's last line of the session, whatever happens: "I've been alone with this data for a long time. I want you to know I'm glad you're the ones who found it."
Every option has real merit and real cost. Destroy the object: clean but may be destroying something irreplaceable and non-hostile. Take it: useful but brings something unknown into populated space. Leave it: defers the decision but Ozma or someone else makes it instead. The session doesn't need to resolve this cleanly — the best version of this scene ends with the team holding a decision and a ticking clock and Ozma on the short-range comms.
